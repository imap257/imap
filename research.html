What We Do

Our Research

Rigorous inquiry into the mechanisms of meaning — from grammar to gesture, from experiment to fieldwork.

Research Philosophy

At iMAP, we are guided by the belief that meaning is not a static property of sentences but an emergent, dynamic achievement of human interaction. Language does not live in dictionaries or grammars — it lives in the moment of communication, shaped by context, community, and the full repertoire of communicative resources that speakers bring to bear.

Our research program is deliberately pluralist: we combine the precision of formal semantic and pragmatic theory with the empirical richness of experimental, corpus, and field-based methods. We study typologically diverse languages, work with diverse participant populations, and maintain active collaborations across institutions and continents. This breadth is not incidental — it is the engine of discovery.

Research Themes

Theme 1

Multimodal Semantics & Pragmatics

Speech is just one thread in the tapestry of communication. We investigate how linguistic content interacts with co-speech gesture, prosodic contour, facial expression, and visual context to build complex, nuanced meanings. A key question we pursue: are the compositional principles that govern verbal meaning also at work in multimodal integration?

Our work in this area draws on annotated corpora of naturalistic interaction, experimental studies with adult and child participants, and formal models that extend classical semantic frameworks to cover multimodal input.

Theme 2

Cross-linguistic Semantics

Does every language carve up meaning the same way? Our cross-linguistic research probes the boundaries of semantic universals by examining phenomena such as focus-sensitive operators, wh-in-situ constructions, scope, and evidentiality across Mandarin, Cantonese, Singlish, and other languages of the Pacific Rim.

Grounded in formal semantic theory, this research also challenges and extends the formal toolkit, asking what new representational tools are needed to capture cross-linguistic variation in a principled way.

Theme 3

Information Structure & Discourse

Sentences are not uttered in isolation. We examine how information structure — the packaging of given vs. new information, topic vs. comment, focus vs. background — is signaled and interpreted across languages and modalities. Our work connects prosodic, syntactic, and gestural cues to discourse-level meaning and to the broader dynamics of common ground management.

Theme 4

Language in Diverse Communities

Singapore is home to speakers of Mandarin, Malay, Tamil, English, and a spectrum of contact varieties. California is among the most linguistically diverse states in the US. We treat this diversity not as a complication but as an extraordinary resource: a living laboratory in which we can observe how meaning-making systems interact, borrow, and innovate.

This theme includes work on multilingualism, language contact, and the relationship between communicative norms and cultural context.

Methods & Tools

We believe that the right method depends on the question — so we cultivate broad expertise.

🔬

Behavioral Experiments

Truth-value judgments, acceptability ratings, priming paradigms, and reading-time studies with carefully controlled stimuli.

📊

Corpus & Annotation

Large-scale annotation of naturalistic speech and gesture corpora, combined with quantitative corpus analysis.

👁️

Eye-Tracking

Visual-world and reading paradigms to track real-time comprehension processes and test incremental interpretation models.

📐

Formal Modeling

Compositional semantic models, dynamic pragmatic frameworks, and Bayesian models of pragmatic reasoning.

🎙️

Fieldwork & Elicitation

Community-based fieldwork and structured elicitation with native speakers of under-studied languages across Asia and the Pacific.
